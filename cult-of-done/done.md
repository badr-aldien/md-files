# DONE

### Building a product on the Cult of Done Manifesto

**Using the BMAD Method** — Brainstorming → PRD → UX

---

## The Process

I used **BMAD** to take an idea from zero to a finished UX, one agent at a time:

1. `bmad-brainstorming` — explore the problem space
2. `bmad-prd` — turn ideas into a real product spec
3. `bmad-ux` — design the actual experience

Each phase fed directly into the next.

---

## Phase 1: Brainstorming

**The seed:** the _Cult of Done Manifesto_ — fighting procrastination and perfectionism by valuing shipping over polishing.

**The twist:** instead of brainstorming features that _help_, I brainstormed the opposite first.

> "How would we make developers procrastinate MORE? How would we make perfectionism WORSE?"

Flipping the problem on its head turned out to be the most useful step in the whole process.

---

## The Evil Product Designer Exercise

Prompt: _imagine you're an evil product designer whose only goal is to make sure developers never ship anything. What's your #1 feature?_

This surfaced anti-patterns across four areas:

- **Workflow traps** — infinite reviews, eternal refactoring
- **Psychology traps** — edge-case paranoia, fake progress
- **Focus traps** — notification floods, context switching
- **Social traps** — fear of judgment, solo perfectionism

Each anti-pattern became a mirror — flip it, and you get a real feature.

---

## From Anti-Pattern to Feature

Once the "worst possible app" was mapped out, the fix became obvious: build the inverse of each trap.

- Endless review cycles → a nudge to ship at "good enough"
- Fake progress → visible, honest progress
- Fear of judgment → a space where shipping is the only metric that matters

This inversion exercise became the backbone of the PRD.

---

## Phase 2: PRD

The brainstorming output fed straight into `bmad-prd`, where the anti-pattern map became real product requirements — built around one core belief from the manifesto:

> **"Failure counts as done."**

The PRD treated _finishing the attempt_ — not the outcome — as the unit of success.

---

## Phase 3: UX — Reinventing the Streak

Classic streak mechanics (Duolingo-style) are powerful but built on **loss aversion** — don't break the chain.

DONE flips that:

- Missing a day isn't failure
- Breaking a streak doesn't erase what happened
- The app literally tells you what you accomplished

> "Streak ended at 12 days. That's 12 days of done. Start the next one."

---

## Why This Matters

The manifesto says done is better than perfect — so the product's own UX had to practice that, not just preach it.

No guilt mechanics. No broken-chain shame. Just an honest count of what got _done_, and a clear nudge to start again.
